> “A healthy man wants a thousand things, a sick man only wants one.”
>
> — Confucius

People often say we have many problems in life - until we get sick. Then we realize we have only one.

**Imagine it is the year 2050.**

A doctor is sitting in front of you. The room feels quiet and heavy. You hear words you never wanted to hear — you have a serious illness. The biggest reason is not bad luck. It is not genetics. It is your lifestyle.

Years of stress. Years of poor sleep. Too much sitting. Too much sugar. Not enough movement. Small choices, repeated every day.

You sit there and think: *What if I could go back?*  
What if I could change my habits?  
What if I could start earlier?

You imagine a second chance. A chance to wake up sooner. To take care of your body. To choose health when it was still easy.

Now,

Imagine you already traveled back in time.  
Imagine this is that second chance.  
You are here. You are reading this.  
Nothing is decided yet.

You can still change the story.

Start today. Fix your habits. Protect your future self. Your future self will thank you!

---

So this article is not really about gym, muscles, or supplements.

It is about awareness.

That scary future is not a fantasy. It is a possible version of life if we keep ignoring simple things. I am not writing this as a doctor or a coach. I am writing this as a normal person who also struggles with discipline, comfort, stress, and daily routine.

This is my story. How sport slowly changed my mindset. How I started training for looks - but stayed for health. And why today I see health as the most important long-term investment.

Sport has always been part of my life. As a kid I did gymnastics, later I played football. In 2012 I started lifting weights, and I immediately liked it. I liked the feeling of getting stronger, building muscle, and being in better shape. I also liked the atmosphere in the gym. Since then, working out has become a constant in my life. Friends played a big role. Gym buddies help a lot when you are just starting. At first you have motivation, but over time motivation fades and discipline takes its place. Consistency is easier when you are not alone.

In my early 20s I trained mostly for appearance. I just enjoyed the process and didn’t think much about the deeper reason behind it. Today my perspective is different. Good shape is nice, but health is much more important.

Your body is the most important system you will ever maintain. Every goal you chase, every relationship you value, and every success you work toward depends on it. Without health, everything else slowly loses meaning.

Another strong reason for me is my children. At the time of writing this, I am a father of two beautiful girls. I want to be strong for them. I want to be their superhero - someone who can protect them and be present in their lives for many years.

My daily routine starts at 7 a.m. I work out four days a week. I rest on Wednesdays and weekends. Most of my training is focused on heavy lifting 🏋️, but now I want to pay more attention to endurance. I plan to add more cardio sessions, maybe on weekends.

🥗 I also try to eat clean. I won’t lie - I am not perfect. I still eat sugar and too many carbs sometimes. But I am trying, and trying already makes a difference. My favorite supplement is creatine. I take 5g every day. I also drink protein shakes quite often. Right now I’m also taking magnesium, omega-3, and vitamin D. For me, this feels like a simple and essential pack for anyone who trains regularly and wants to have more energy, recover better, and see steady progress. In the last few months, I also started using electrolyte powder during workouts to stay hydrated. I see it more as a supportive supplement, not a foundation like creatine, magnesium, vitamin D, or omega-3.

Sleep must be the number one priority. It is probably the most underrated free "tool" you have to stay healthy. Unfortunately, my sleep is not great right now 😴 my second baby is only a few months old. But if you don’t have a special reason like this, do everything you can to improve your sleep. It makes a huge difference.

After my workout and morning routine, I spend most of the day sitting and working. This worries me. Sitting 8–10 hours a day is not healthy, no matter how hard you train in the morning. This is the next thing I want to improve. I am thinking about buying a standing desk and adding short walks or mini workouts every 2–3 hours.

The most important thing is realizing the value of health early. I am glad I started thinking about it now, not at 60. But even if you are 60 or older, it is never too late.

> “The best time to plant a tree was 20 years ago. The second best time is now.”

Nothing feels worse than regret - especially regret about something you could have done but didn’t. The regret of not taking care of yourself hurts much more than the discomfort of discipline. Waking up early, going to bed on time, eating better, and training regularly can be hard. But it is still much easier than living with regret later.
